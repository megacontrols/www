Air-Conditioning and Refrigeration Institute, USA The Air-Conditioning and Refrigeration (ARI) is the national trade association representing manufacturers of more than 90 percent of North American produced central air conditioning and commercial refrigeration equipment. ARI, a major voice for the air conditioning industry, establishes product standards, principally performance rating standards and administers voluntary performance certification programs for industry products by testing products to verify and correct performance ratings and publishes certified rating data to help assure customers receive products that perform as claimed by the manufacturers. American National Standards Institute, USA The American National Standards Institute (ANSI) has served in its capacity as administrator and coordinator of the United States private sector voluntary standardization system for more than 80 years. Founded in 1918 by five engineering societies and three government agencies, the Institute remains a private, nonprofit membership organization supported by a diverse constituency of private and public sector organizations. 
ANSI does not itself develop American National Standards (ANSs); rather it facilitates development by establishing consensus among qualified groups. The Institute ensures that its guiding principles -- consensus, due process and openness -- are followed by the more than 175 distinct entities currently accredited under one of the Federation’s three methods of accreditation (organization, committee or canvass). In 1999 alone the number of American National Standards increased by nearly 5.5% to a new total of 14,650 approved ANS. ANSI-accredited developers are committed to supporting the development of national and, in many cases international standards, addressing the critical trends of technological innovation, marketplace globalization and regulatory reform.

Professional Engineers Ontario, Canada Engineering in Ontario is a self-regulated profession under the Professional Engineers Act. Professional Engineers Ontario (PEO) is the body that administers the Act. Similar licensing bodies exist in all Canadian provinces and territories. The legislation defines profesional engineering in terms of safeguarding life, health, property and public welfare.
In Canada, professional engineers must be licensed by a provincial or territorial regulatory licensing body, such as Professional Engineers Ontario. Only holders of a valid P.Eng. licence are legally permitted to use the term "engineer" in their job title, or any other term, title or description that may lead to the belief that they are authorized to practise professional engineering. Only licence holders are allowed to use the title P.Eng. after their names. To use the P.Eng. title and be able to take responsibility for professional engineering work, you must pass an examination on engineering law and ethics and obtain acceptable work experience. For more than 60 years, the title "P.Eng." or "profesional engineer" has identified licensed practitioners who have technical expertise, high standards of professionalism and, above all, a responsibility to safeguard the public interest where engineering is concerned.
